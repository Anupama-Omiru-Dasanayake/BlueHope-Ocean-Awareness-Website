Life Below Water

Coral Reefs Loss

Our oceans' most vital ecosystems are vanishing. Coral reefs support 25% of all marine life, yet they face an unprecedented existential crisis due to human activity and climate shifts.

Understanding Climate Impact

The ocean plays a vital role in maintaining Earth's climate and supporting marine life. It regulates global temperatures, produces a significant portion of the oxygen we breathe, and provides habitats for millions of species. However, climate change is placing enormous pressure on ocean ecosystems.

Human activities release large amounts of greenhouse gases into the atmosphere. These gases trap heat and cause global temperatures to rise. The ocean absorbs much of this excess heat and carbon dioxide, which leads to several environmental changes including ocean warming, coral bleaching, ocean acidification, and rising sea levels.

Destructive Fishing Practices

Unsustainable fishing methods represent a direct physical threat to coral structures. Cyanide fishing, blast fishing with explosives, and bottom trawling physically demolish centuries-old reef formations in seconds, leaving behind barren "rubble zones" where life cannot easily return.

Beyond physical destruction, overfishing of key species disrupts the delicate ecological balance. When herbivorous fish are removed, algae can overgrow and smother the coral, preventing new larvae from settling and effectively killing the remaining reef through competition for space and light.

Ocean Acidification

As the ocean absorbs roughly 30% of the carbon dioxide released into the atmosphere, its chemistry changes. This process, known as ocean acidification, reduces the availability of carbonate ions—the essential building blocks corals need to construct their calcium carbonate skeletons.

When acidity levels rise, the rate of reef calcification slows down. In extreme cases, the water can become so corrosive that it literally begins to dissolve existing reef structures. This makes reefs more fragile, less able to withstand storm damage, and slower to recover from other environmental stressors.

The Crisis of Coral Bleaching

Corals share a symbiotic relationship with tiny algae called zooxanthellae, which live in their tissues and provide them with food and vibrant color. When sea temperatures rise even by 1–2 degrees Celsius above normal, corals become stressed and expel these algae.

Without the algae, the coral turns ghostly white—a state known as bleaching. While a bleached coral is not yet dead, it is under extreme stress and begins to starve. If temperatures don't return to normal quickly, the coral will die, leading to the collapse of the entire ecosystem that depends on it.

Impact Statistics

Ocean Heat Absorption

The ocean absorbs about 90% of the excess heat generated by global warming, causing rising ocean temperatures.

50%

Coral Reefs at Risk

Around 50% of the world's coral reefs are already threatened by rising ocean temperatures and climate-related stress.

25%

Rising Sea Levels

Global sea levels are rising by approximately 3.3 millimeters per year, mainly due to melting glaciers.

Reducing the impact of climate change requires global cooperation as well as individual actions. Governments and organizations must work together to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and transition to cleaner energy sources.

By working together and making responsible choices, we can help protect marine ecosystems and preserve life below water for future generations.
